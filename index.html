1) Definitions

1.1 What is an NGO?

In UN ECOSOC(United Nations Economic and Social Council) consultative arrangements, an organization ‚Äúnot established by a governmental entity or intergovernmental agreement‚Äù is considered a non-governmental organization for the purpose of those arrangements. (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF‚ÄîUN DESA)) [web:63]

ECOSOC also specifies governance-related expectations for NGOs seeking consultative status (e.g., representative structure and accountability mechanisms to members). (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF‚ÄîUN DESA)) [web:63]

Working definition: An NGO is a non-state, non-profit (or not-for-profit) organization, institutionally independent of government creation, that undertakes social, humanitarian, development, rights, or environmental work and may engage with public systems through coordination or consultative mechanisms. (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF‚ÄîUN DESA)) [web:63]

1.2 What is a ‚Äúdisaster‚Äù (India legal definition)?

Under India‚Äôs Disaster Management Act, 2005, ‚Äúdisaster‚Äù means a catastrophe/mishap/calamity/grave occurrence arising from natural or man-made causes (or accident/negligence) that results in substantial loss of life/suffering/property damage/environmental degradation and is beyond the coping capacity of the affected community. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF‚ÄîNDM India)) [web:74]

1.3 What is ‚Äúdisaster management‚Äù (India legal definition)?

The DM Act defines ‚Äúdisaster management‚Äù as a continuous and integrated process of planning, organizing, coordinating, and implementing measures for prevention, mitigation, capacity-building, preparedness, prompt response, assessment, evacuation/rescue/relief, rehabilitation, and reconstruction. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF‚ÄîNDM India)) [web:74]

1.4 Core DRR terminology (international standard)

Hazard: A process, phenomenon, or human activity that may cause loss of life/injury/health impacts, property damage, social/economic disruption, or environmental degradation. (UNDRR terminology‚ÄîHazard) [web:86]

Disaster risk: The potential loss of life, injury, or destroyed/damaged assets that could occur in a period of time, probabilistically as a function of hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity. (UNDRR terminology‚ÄîDisaster risk) [web:88]

Disaster risk reduction (DRR): Aimed at preventing new and reducing existing disaster risk and managing residual risk, strengthening resilience and supporting sustainable development. (UNDRR terminology‚ÄîDRR) [web:75]

Resilience: The ability of a system/community/society exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, accommodate, adapt to, transform, and recover from hazard effects in a timely and efficient manner, including preserving/restoring essential structures/functions through risk management. (UNDRR terminology‚ÄîResilience) [web:78]

1.5 International policy frame: Sendai

The Sendai Framework (2015‚Äì2030) is the global DRR framework and calls for engaging relevant stakeholders (including community practitioners, volunteers, and vulnerable groups) in DRR design and implementation. (Sendai Framework (PDF on PreventionWeb)) [web:13]

A UNDRR explainer page can be cited in short lecture notes and policy introductions. (UNDRR‚ÄîWhat is the Sendai Framework?) [web:10]

2) Why NGOs are central in disaster management

2.1 Complementarity with the state

India‚Äôs legal framing makes disaster management an integrated, continuous process requiring planning, coordination, and implementation across prevention-to-reconstruction, which creates a space for specialized and community-linked actors to supplement government capacity. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF‚ÄîNDM India)) [web:74]

NDMA‚Äôs dedicated guideline on the ‚ÄúRole of NGOs‚Äù provides an official basis to treat NGOs as structured partners (not ad-hoc actors) across preparedness, response, and recovery, including coordination expectations. (NDMA‚ÄîRole of NGOs Guideline (PDF)) [web:3]

2.2 CBDRR lens (community-first)

NDMA‚Äôs CBDRR Guidelines explicitly frame CSOs/NGOs and CBOs, alongside PRIs, as key actors in transforming communities toward self-reliance in dealing with disasters. (NDMA‚ÄîCBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF)) [web:28]

2.3 Governance and legitimacy (UN perspective)

ECOSOC consultative arrangements emphasize that NGOs should have representative structures and accountability mechanisms, which is relevant when discussing NGO legitimacy in disaster governance. (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF‚ÄîUN DESA)) [web:63]

3) Official & international policy anchors (where NGO roles are defined)

India (official)

NDMA‚Äôs guideline explicitly focuses on NGO roles across the disaster management cycle and GO‚ÄìNGO coordination mechanisms. (NDMA Guideline‚ÄîRole of NGOs in Disaster Management (PDF)) [web:3]

The CBDRR Guidelines (2024) frame NGOs/CSOs and CBOs as essential actors alongside PRIs for building self-reliant communities. (NDMA‚ÄîCBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF)) [web:28]

Odisha (official)

OSDMA maintains a GO‚ÄìNGO coordination focus and highlights NGOs‚Äô grassroots presence and community linkages in disaster contexts. (OSDMA‚ÄîGO‚ÄìNGO Coordination) [web:5]

OSDMA also provides an official NGO registration interface for coordination and information capture (operational areas, prior disaster work, contacts). (OSDMA‚ÄîNGO Registration) [web:32]

The Sendai Framework stresses that governments should engage relevant stakeholders (including community practitioners, volunteers, vulnerable groups) across DRR priorities. (Sendai Framework (PDF on PreventionWeb)) [web:13]
A concise UNDRR explainer is also available for policy framing in academic writing. (UNDRR‚ÄîWhat is the Sendai Framework?) [web:10]

4) Statistics (India + Odisha)

India: hazard exposure (official synthesis)

India‚Äôs national Sendai Midterm Review report compiles headline exposure indicators (e.g., large shares of land/coastline exposed to earthquakes, floods, cyclones/tsunami risks, and drought vulnerability). (NDMA‚ÄîIndia Sendai Framework Midterm Review (VNR) (PDF)) [web:51]

Use the NDMA VNR as your ‚Äúofficial umbrella source‚Äù for nationally reported vulnerability statistics in academic writing, and cite the relevant page/section after you verify the exact line you are quoting. (NDMA‚ÄîIndia Sendai Framework Midterm Review (VNR) (PDF)) [web:51]

Odisha: evidence of risk-reduction outcomes

Odisha‚Äôs preparedness improvements (institutional strengthening and evacuation/shelter systems) are highlighted by the World Bank as globally instructive. (World Bank‚ÄîOdisha‚Äôs turnaround) [web:46]

Cyclone Phailin (2013) is documented with emphasis on coordinated response and volunteer mobilization linked to DRM programming. (UNDP‚ÄîPhailin response assessment (PDF)) [web:20]

5) NGO roles across the Disaster Management Cycle

NDMA‚Äôs NGO guideline frames NGO contributions across preparedness, mitigation, response, and recovery, including community awareness, training, relief logistics, and longer-term rehabilitation. (NDMA Guideline‚ÄîRole of NGOs (PDF)) [web:3]

The CBDRR Guidelines emphasize community-centered methods where NGOs/CSOs help enable risk understanding, inclusive planning, local institutions (committees/volunteers), and continuity between DRR and development. (NDMA‚ÄîCBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF)) [web:28]

5.1 Preparedness (typical NGO functions)

  • Community organization and volunteer training, mock drills, awareness/IEC, school/community preparedness support. (NDMA‚ÄîCBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF)) [web:28]
  • Risk communication and community media approaches are studied in Odisha (e.g., community radio and disaster communication). (SAGE‚ÄîCommunity radio & disaster communication in Odisha (2025)) [web:2]

5.2 Mitigation and DRR mainstreaming

  • Participatory risk assessment and mainstreaming DRR in local development planning (CBDRR framing). (NDMA‚ÄîCBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF)) [web:28]
  • Integration with national/state planning is also tied to government‚ÄìUNDP program approaches for institutional/community resilience. (MHA/NDM India‚ÄîGoI‚ÄìUNDP Projects) [web:6]

5.3 Response (relief + coordination)

  • Rapid needs assessment, relief distribution (WASH/food/shelter), protection of vulnerable groups, and coordination with government systems. (NDMA Guideline‚ÄîRole of NGOs (PDF)) [web:3]
  • For Phailin, UNDP‚Äôs review documents volunteer mobilization and DRM-linked community engagement during response. (UNDP‚ÄîPhailin assessment (PDF)) [web:20]

5.4 Recovery and reconstruction

  • ‚ÄúBuild back better‚Äù support, livelihood restoration, psychosocial support, and strengthening local institutions to reduce future risk. (NDMA Guideline‚ÄîRole of NGOs (PDF)) [web:3]

9) Example

NGOs are formally recognized in India‚Äôs disaster governance as agencies that supplement government capacity across preparedness, response, and recovery, and NDMA provides detailed guidance on operational roles and coordination expectations. (NDMA Guideline‚ÄîRole of NGOs (PDF)) [web:3]
In Odisha, institutional investments and structured coordination are widely cited as central to the state‚Äôs improved cyclone outcomes, with external assessments framing Odisha‚Äôs approach as globally instructive for disaster resilience-building. (World Bank‚ÄîOdisha turnaround) [web:46]
Community-centered DRR approaches further position NGOs/CSOs and CBOs as core actors in enabling local risk understanding, inclusive planning, and preparedness capacity, as codified in India‚Äôs CBDRR Guidelines. (NDMA‚ÄîCBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF)) [web:28]

10) Quick revision

  • NGO (UN consultative framing): Not established by a governmental entity or intergovernmental agreement (for consultative arrangements), with governance/accountability expectations. (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF)) [web:63]
  • Disaster (India): A grave occurrence causing substantial losses and beyond community coping capacity. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF)) [web:74]
  • Disaster management (India): Continuous, integrated process from prevention to reconstruction. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF)) [web:74]
  • Hazard: A potentially damaging process/phenomenon/human activity. (UNDRR‚ÄîHazard) [web:86]
  • Disaster risk: Function of hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity. (UNDRR‚ÄîDisaster risk) [web:88]
  • DRR: Prevent new risk, reduce existing risk, manage residual risk. (UNDRR‚ÄîDRR) [web:75]
  • Resilience: Resist‚Äìabsorb‚Äìadapt/transform‚Äìrecover while preserving essential functions. (UNDRR‚ÄîResilience) [web:78]
