    I. Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)

    1. Which Act provides the legal framework for Disaster Management in India?
    2. a) The Environment Protection Act, 1986
    3. b) The Disaster Management Act, 2005
    4. c) The Epidemic Diseases Act, 1897
    5. d) The Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage Act, 2010
    View Answer
    Answer: (b) The Disaster Management Act, 2005
    Explanation: The Disaster Management Act, 2005 is the primary law in India that provides the institutional and legal framework for disaster management at the national, state, and district levels.
    1. The Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction applies to which time period?
    2. a) 2005–2015
    3. b) 2010–2020
    4. c) 2015–2030
    5. d) 2020–2040
    View Answer
    Answer: (c) 2015–2030
    Explanation: The Sendai Framework was adopted in 2015 and outlines the global roadmap for disaster risk reduction for the 15-year period from 2015 to 2030.
    1. Which cyclone is often cited as a turning point for Odisha’s disaster preparedness mechanics?
    2. a) Cyclone Amphan (2020)
    3. b) Cyclone Fani (2019)
    4. c) Cyclone Phailin (2013)
    5. d) 1999 Super Cyclone
    View Answer
    Answer: (d) 1999 Super Cyclone
    Explanation: The devastation caused by the 1999 Super Cyclone led to the creation of OSDMA and a complete overhaul of Odisha's disaster management machinery, marking the turning point.
    1. Which organization acts as the primary agency for GO–NGO coordination in Odisha?
    2. a) UNDP
    3. b) OSDMA
    4. c) ODMP
    5. d) Indian Red Cross Society
    View Answer
    Answer: (b) OSDMA
    Explanation: The Odisha State Disaster Management Authority (OSDMA) is the official state nodal agency that facilitates and oversees GO–NGO coordination during disasters.
    1. "Build Back Better" is a core principle associated with which phase of the Disaster Management Cycle?
    2. a) Mitigation
    3. b) Preparedness
    4. c) Response
    5. d) Recovery and Reconstruction
    View Answer
    Answer: (d) Recovery and Reconstruction
    Explanation: "Build Back Better" is a reconstruction philosophy used in the recovery phase to ensure that new infrastructure is more resilient to future disasters than what existed before.

    II. Fill in the Blanks

    1. ____ is defined as the ability of a system, community, or society exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, accommodate, adapt to, transform, and recover from the effects of a hazard.
    View Answer
    Answer: Resilience
    Explanation: This is the standard UNDRR definition of resilience, emphasizing the capacity to bounce back and maintain essential functions.
    1. A ____ is a process, phenomenon, or human activity that may cause loss of life, injury, or other health impacts, property damage, social and economic disruption, or environmental degradation.
    View Answer
    Answer: Hazard
    Explanation: A hazard is the potentially damaging event or phenomenon itself (like a cyclone or earthquake), distinct from the risk or disaster it may cause.
    1. ____ aims at preventing new and reducing existing disaster risk and managing residual risk.
    View Answer
    Answer: Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR)
    Explanation: DRR is the systematic approach to identifying, assessing, and reducing the risks of disaster, rather than just managing the event after it happens.
    1. The ____ (Network) was established in Odisha after the 1999 Super Cyclone to coordinate NGO efforts.
    View Answer
    Answer: ODMP (Odisha Development Management Programme)
    Explanation: ODMP was formed by NGOs in the aftermath of the 1999 cyclone to ensure better coordination and collective action among civil society organizations.

    1. NGOs are government entities established by an act of Parliament. (True/False)
    View Answer
    Answer: False
    Explanation: By definition, Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs) are independent of government control and are not created by the government, though they may partner with it.
    1. The Disaster Management Act, 2005 defines "disaster management" as a continuous and integrated process. (True/False)
    View Answer
    Answer: True
    Explanation: The Act specifically defines disaster management not as a one-time event response, but as a continuous cycle including planning, organizing, and implementing measures.
    1. Community-Based Disaster Risk Reduction (CBDRR) excludes the participation of local communities and focuses only on central planning. (True/False)
    View Answer
    Answer: False
    Explanation: CBDRR is fundamentally about *including* local communities in the planning and decision-making process to make them self-reliant.
    1. OSDMA provides an official registration interface for NGOs to facilitate coordination during disasters. (True/False)
    View Answer
    Answer: True
    Explanation: OSDMA's website maintains a registration portal specifically to map NGO capabilities and contact details for effective coordination during emergencies.

    V. Assertion and Reason

    Directions: For the following questions, two statements are given-one labeled Assertion (A) and the other labeled Reason (R). Select the correct answer to these questions from the codes (a), (b), (c) and (d) as given below:

    • a) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A.
    • b) Both A and R are true but R is NOT the correct explanation of A.
    • c) A is true but R is false.

    • d) A is false but R is true.

    • Assertion (A): NGOs play a critical complementary role to the government in disaster management. Reason (R): Government machinery may sometimes need specialized support, community linkages, and additional manpower which NGOs can provide effectively.

    View Answer
    Answer: (a) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A.
    Explanation: The assertion is true because governments cannot do everything alone. The reason correctly explains *why* NGOs are complementary: they fill specific gaps like grassroots reach and specialized skills that the government might lack in a crisis.
    1. Assertion (A): The Sendai Framework calls for an all-of-society engagement in disaster risk reduction. Reason (R): Disaster risk reduction is solely the responsibility of the central government.
    View Answer
    Answer: (c) A is true but R is false.
    Explanation: The assertion is true as Sendai emphasizes shared responsibility. The reason is false because DRR is a shared responsibility involving states, local bodies, private sector, NGOs, and communities, not just the central government.

    • Define NGO (Non-Governmental Organization), disaster, disaster management, hazard, disaster risk, DRR (Disaster Risk Reduction), and resilience using standard official sources. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF—NDM India)) (UNDRR terminology—Hazard) (UNDRR terminology—Disaster risk) (UNDRR terminology—DRR) (UNDRR terminology—Resilience)
    • Explain the roles of NGOs across the disaster management cycle using India’s national guidance and CBDRR (Community-Based Disaster Risk Reduction) framing. (NDMA—Role of NGOs Guideline (PDF)) (NDMA—CBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF))

    1) Definitions

    1.1 What is an NGO?

    In UN ECOSOC(United Nations Economic and Social Council) consultative arrangements, an organization “not established by a governmental entity or intergovernmental agreement” is considered a non-governmental organization for the purpose of those arrangements. (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF—UN DESA))

    ECOSOC also specifies governance-related expectations for NGOs seeking consultative status (e.g., representative structure and accountability mechanisms to members). (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF—UN DESA))

    Working definition: An NGO is a non-state, non-profit (or not-for-profit) organization, institutionally independent of government creation, that undertakes social, humanitarian, development, rights, or environmental work and may engage with public systems through coordination or consultative mechanisms. (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF—UN DESA))

    1.2 What is a “disaster” (India legal definition)?

    Under India’s Disaster Management Act, 2005, “disaster” means a catastrophe/mishap/calamity/grave occurrence arising from natural or man-made causes (or accident/negligence) that results in substantial loss of life/suffering/property damage/environmental degradation and is beyond the coping capacity of the affected community. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF—NDM India))

    1.3 What is “disaster management” (India legal definition)?

    The DM Act defines “disaster management” as a continuous and integrated process of planning, organizing, coordinating, and implementing measures for prevention, mitigation, capacity-building, preparedness, prompt response, assessment, evacuation/rescue/relief, rehabilitation, and reconstruction. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF—NDM India))

    1.4 Core DRR terminology (international standard)

    Hazard: A process, phenomenon, or human activity that may cause loss of life/injury/health impacts, property damage, social/economic disruption, or environmental degradation. (UNDRR terminology—Hazard)

    Disaster risk: The potential loss of life, injury, or destroyed/damaged assets that could occur in a period of time, probabilistically as a function of hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity. (UNDRR terminology—Disaster risk)

    Disaster risk reduction (DRR): Aimed at preventing new and reducing existing disaster risk and managing residual risk, strengthening resilience and supporting sustainable development. (UNDRR terminology—DRR)

    Resilience: The ability of a system/community/society exposed to hazards to resist, absorb, accommodate, adapt to, transform, and recover from hazard effects in a timely and efficient manner, including preserving/restoring essential structures/functions through risk management. (UNDRR terminology—Resilience)

    1.5 International policy frame: Sendai

    The Sendai Framework (2015–2030) is the global DRR framework and calls for engaging relevant stakeholders (including community practitioners, volunteers, and vulnerable groups) in DRR design and implementation. (Sendai Framework (PDF on PreventionWeb))

    A UNDRR explainer page can be cited in short lecture notes and policy introductions. (UNDRR—What is the Sendai Framework?)

    2) Why NGOs are central in disaster management

    2.1 Complementarity with the state

    India’s legal framing makes disaster management an integrated, continuous process requiring planning, coordination, and implementation across prevention-to-reconstruction, which creates a space for specialized and community-linked actors to supplement government capacity. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF—NDM India))

    NDMA’s dedicated guideline on the “Role of NGOs” provides an official basis to treat NGOs as structured partners (not ad-hoc actors) across preparedness, response, and recovery, including coordination expectations. (NDMA—Role of NGOs Guideline (PDF))

    2.2 CBDRR lens (community-first)

    NDMA’s CBDRR Guidelines explicitly frame CSOs/NGOs and CBOs, alongside PRIs, as key actors in transforming communities toward self-reliance in dealing with disasters. (NDMA—CBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF))

    2.3 Governance and legitimacy (UN perspective)

    ECOSOC consultative arrangements emphasize that NGOs should have representative structures and accountability mechanisms, which is relevant when discussing NGO legitimacy in disaster governance. (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF—UN DESA))

    3) Official & international policy anchors (where NGO roles are defined)

    India (official)

    NDMA’s guideline explicitly focuses on NGO roles across the disaster management cycle and GO–NGO coordination mechanisms. (NDMA Guideline—Role of NGOs in Disaster Management (PDF))

    The CBDRR Guidelines (2024) frame NGOs/CSOs and CBOs as essential actors alongside PRIs for building self-reliant communities. (NDMA—CBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF))

    Odisha (official)

    OSDMA maintains a GO–NGO coordination focus and highlights NGOs’ grassroots presence and community linkages in disaster contexts. (OSDMA—GO–NGO Coordination)

    OSDMA also provides an official NGO registration interface for coordination and information capture (operational areas, prior disaster work, contacts). (OSDMA—NGO Registration)

    International (official)

    The Sendai Framework stresses that governments should engage relevant stakeholders (including community practitioners, volunteers, vulnerable groups) across DRR priorities. (Sendai Framework (PDF on PreventionWeb)) A concise UNDRR explainer is also available for policy framing in academic writing. (UNDRR—What is the Sendai Framework?)

    4) Statistics (India + Odisha)

    India: hazard exposure (official synthesis)

    India’s national Sendai Midterm Review report compiles headline exposure indicators (e.g., large shares of land/coastline exposed to earthquakes, floods, cyclones/tsunami risks, and drought vulnerability). (NDMA—India Sendai Framework Midterm Review (VNR) (PDF))

    Use the NDMA VNR as your “official umbrella source” for nationally reported vulnerability statistics in academic writing, and cite the relevant page/section after you verify the exact line you are quoting. (NDMA—India Sendai Framework Midterm Review (VNR) (PDF))

    Odisha: evidence of risk-reduction outcomes

    Odisha’s preparedness improvements (institutional strengthening and evacuation/shelter systems) are highlighted by the World Bank as globally instructive. (World Bank—Odisha’s turnaround)

    Cyclone Phailin (2013) is documented with emphasis on coordinated response and volunteer mobilization linked to DRM programming. (UNDP—Phailin response assessment (PDF))

    For an academic narrative on Phailin and Odisha preparedness, see the dedicated case study paper. (UKSCIP paper—Phailin cyclone case study (PDF))

    5) NGO roles across the Disaster Management Cycle

    NDMA’s NGO guideline frames NGO contributions across preparedness, mitigation, response, and recovery, including community awareness, training, relief logistics, and longer-term rehabilitation. (NDMA Guideline—Role of NGOs (PDF))

    The CBDRR Guidelines emphasize community-centered methods where NGOs/CSOs help enable risk understanding, inclusive planning, local institutions (committees/volunteers), and continuity between DRR and development. (NDMA—CBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF))

    5.1 Preparedness (typical NGO functions)

    • Community organization and volunteer training, mock drills, awareness/IEC, school/community preparedness support. (NDMA—CBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF))
    • Risk communication and community media approaches are studied in Odisha (e.g., community radio and disaster communication). (SAGE—Community radio & disaster communication in Odisha (2025))

    5.2 Mitigation and DRR mainstreaming

    • Participatory risk assessment and mainstreaming DRR in local development planning (CBDRR framing). (NDMA—CBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF))
    • Integration with national/state planning is also tied to government–UNDP program approaches for institutional/community resilience. (MHA/NDM India—GoI–UNDP Projects)

    5.3 Response (relief + coordination)

    • Rapid needs assessment, relief distribution (WASH/food/shelter), protection of vulnerable groups, and coordination with government systems. (NDMA Guideline—Role of NGOs (PDF))
    • For Phailin, UNDP’s review documents volunteer mobilization and DRM-linked community engagement during response. (UNDP—Phailin assessment (PDF))

    5.4 Recovery and reconstruction

    • “Build back better” support, livelihood restoration, psychosocial support, and strengthening local institutions to reduce future risk. (NDMA Guideline—Role of NGOs (PDF))

    9) Example

    NGOs are formally recognized in India’s disaster governance as agencies that supplement government capacity across preparedness, response, and recovery, and NDMA provides detailed guidance on operational roles and coordination expectations. (NDMA Guideline—Role of NGOs (PDF)) In Odisha, institutional investments and structured coordination are widely cited as central to the state’s improved cyclone outcomes, with external assessments framing Odisha’s approach as globally instructive for disaster resilience-building. (World Bank—Odisha turnaround) Community-centered DRR approaches further position NGOs/CSOs and CBOs as core actors in enabling local risk understanding, inclusive planning, and preparedness capacity, as codified in India’s CBDRR Guidelines. (NDMA—CBDRR Guidelines Oct 2024 (PDF))

    10) Quick revision

    • NGO (UN consultative framing): Not established by a governmental entity or intergovernmental agreement (for consultative arrangements), with governance/accountability expectations. (ECOSOC Resolution 1996/31 (PDF))
    • Disaster (India): A grave occurrence causing substantial losses and beyond community coping capacity. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF))
    • Disaster management (India): Continuous, integrated process from prevention to reconstruction. (Disaster Management Act, 2005 (PDF))
    • Hazard: A potentially damaging process/phenomenon/human activity. (UNDRR—Hazard)
    • Disaster risk: Function of hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and capacity. (UNDRR—Disaster risk)
    • DRR: Prevent new risk, reduce existing risk, manage residual risk. (UNDRR—DRR)
    • Resilience: Resist–absorb–adapt/transform–recover while preserving essential functions. (UNDRR—Resilience)
